Part One

How We Got Here

AI did not improve gradually. It moved in sudden leaps separated by long, quiet decades. Watch the pacing as you scroll: the first eighty years pass in a moment. Then 2017 arrives, and time slows down.

1936

Turing’s Question

Alan Turing publishes “On Computable Numbers” and later asks the question that launches a field: can a machine think?

1956

The Dartmouth Conference

The term “artificial intelligence” is coined. Researchers predict human-level AI within a decade. It will take seventy years longer than that.

1966–1987

The AI Winters

Funding collapses twice as AI fails to deliver on its promises. Hype, then disappointment, then silence. The field nearly dies. Twice.

Decades pass. Almost nothing happens.

1997

Deep Blue Beats Kasparov

IBM’s chess computer defeats the world champion. Machines can master one narrow task. General intelligence remains a fantasy.

2012

The Deep Learning Breakthrough

AlexNet wins an image recognition contest by a stunning margin. Neural networks, long dismissed, suddenly work. The modern era begins here.

Then everything changes.

The Foundation
2017

“Attention Is All You Need”

Eight Google researchers publish the Transformer architecture. This single paper is the technical foundation of every major AI model that follows: GPT, Claude, Gemini, all of it. Most of the world has never heard of it.

The Public Moment
2022

ChatGPT Launches

One hundred million users in two months, the fastest product adoption in history. For most people, this is the day AI became real.

The Race
2023

The Frontier Labs Multiply

Anthropic releases Claude. Google answers with Gemini. Meta open-sources Llama. The race is no longer one company. Capabilities begin doubling roughly every six months.

From Talking to Building
2024

Vibe Coding Arrives

Models become good enough to write working software from plain-language descriptions. People who have never written a line of code start shipping real tools. The ceiling on what one person can build rises dramatically.

The Jump
Late 2025

Something Shifts

A new generation of models lands, led by releases like Claude Opus 4.5 and Gemini 3, and early adopters notice that AI-assisted building has taken a qualitative leap. Not just faster. Categorically more capable. The tools start to feel different.

A Side Project
Nov 2025

A Developer Ships a Lobster

Austrian developer Peter Steinberger quietly publishes a side project to GitHub: a free, open-source AI agent that runs on your own computer. You text it a task. It gets the task done. For two months, almost nobody notices.

The last week of January 2026

Then the internet found it.

The project, by then called OpenClaw, became the fastest-growing open-source project in GitHub history. For the first time, a mass audience saw what an AI agent actually is: not a chatbot, but a system that acts on your behalf.

Why this moment matters

OpenClaw did not go viral because of marketing. It went viral because it worked. Chatbots could summarize, draft, and answer, but you still had to copy, paste, click, and send everything yourself. An agent collapses that entire loop. It feels less like talking to software and more like handing work to a capable assistant. That shift, from AI that talks to AI that acts, is the moment we are living in right now.

We are not watching AI improve.
We are watching it change what’s possible.

Part Two

The Current Landscape

Three labs sit at the frontier, and one company you already work for puts their models inside the tools you use all day. The labs release new flagship models every few months, so specific names age fast. What matters is knowing who the players are and what each one is for.

Anthropic

The trust-focused lab

Founded by former OpenAI researchers with safety as the founding premise. Maker of Claude, widely regarded as the strongest model family for nuanced reasoning, long documents, and writing quality.

  • Claude.ai · web and mobile chat
  • Claude Desktop · AI with access to your files, calendar, and apps
  • Claude Cowork · an agentic desktop app for knowledge work
  • Claude Code · an agent for software work
Current flagships: the Claude 5 family arrived in June 2026, alongside the Claude 4 line (Opus, Sonnet, Haiku).

OpenAI

The lab that started the wave

Creator of ChatGPT and the GPT series. The largest user base, the most recognized brand, and the deepest integration into third-party tools across the industry.

  • ChatGPT · web, mobile, and desktop chat
  • Codex · an autonomous coding agent that works in the background
  • OpenAI API · powers thousands of other products
Current flagship: GPT-5.5, released April 2026. OpenAI also hired OpenClaw’s creator to build next-generation agents.

Google DeepMind

The everywhere lab

Google’s consolidated AI arm. Its superpower is distribution: Gemini is woven directly into Docs, Sheets, Gmail, and Search, the tools most school systems already live in.

  • Gemini · chat at gemini.google.com and inside Workspace
  • NotebookLM · a standout research tool that works only from sources you give it
  • Gemini Spark · a cloud-based agent that works around the clock
Current flagship: Gemini 3.1 Pro, with the fast and inexpensive 3.5 Flash beside it.

Microsoft Copilot

The workplace layer

Not a fourth frontier lab. Microsoft’s play is different: take frontier models, from OpenAI, Anthropic, and increasingly its own, and install them inside Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams, and PowerPoint. You do not go to the AI. The AI is already in your tools.

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot · AI inside the Office apps: draft in Word, analyze in Excel, summarize in Teams, triage in Outlook
  • Copilot Chat · a general assistant that can reason over your work files, email, and meetings
  • Copilot Studio · build custom agents for your organization without code

Why your employer points you here

When you use Copilot through a work account, your prompts and files stay inside your organization’s protected environment. They are not used to train models, and they are covered by your org’s data agreements with Microsoft. Remember the “what happens next depends on the tool” step coming up in the responsible use section: Copilot under a work account is the approved-tool side of that line. That is why many organizations, including ours, make it the default for work containing sensitive content.

The honest tradeoff: Copilot is the safest place for client work, while the standalone lab apps often get new capabilities first. Knowing both is the point of this page.

Part Three

What This Unlocked for Knowledge Workers

Each wave of capability stacked on the last. None of the earlier layers went away. They compounded. And for our work, right now, one layer matters more than the rest.

2022–23

Search & Synthesis

AI reads documents, summarizes research, and answers questions in plain language. Intake and orientation that took an hour takes minutes.

2023–24

Writing & Drafting

AI produces professional first drafts of memos, proposals, and frameworks. The blank page disappears. Your job shifts from construction to editing and judgment.

2024–25

BuildingThe unlock · case study below ↓

AI generates working code, interactive tools, and data visualizations. Consultants who cannot code now ship things that used to require a developer. For education consultants, this is the unlock of the moment: the shortest path from AI curiosity to something your clients can actually hold, click, and use. Master this layer and the others come along for free.

2025–now

ActingWorth watching

AI agents execute multi-step workflows across apps with minimal supervision. OpenClaw made the potential vivid, and tools like Claude Desktop are making it practical. This layer is arriving fast and getting genuinely useful. It is also not where most of us will start. Start one layer up.

Part Four

Working Responsibly

We work with student data, district data, and client information that carries legal and ethical weight. AI tools are powerful, but what you type into them leaves your computer. What you put in matters.

You type something into an AI tool

A question, a draft, a document. Whatever it is, it does not stay on your screen.

It travels to the company’s servers

Your text is processed by computers you do not control, governed by that tool’s data policies, not yours.

What happens next depends on the tool

Approved enterprise accounts carry data protections and agreements; Copilot through your work account is the clearest example. Free consumer accounts often do not, and some use your inputs to train future models.

The task is rarely the problem.
The information you share is.

Asking AI to summarize meeting notes is fine. Asking it to summarize meeting notes that contain student names is not. Same task, different inputs, completely different answer. So the question to ask is never “is AI allowed?” It is “is this information safe to send to an external service?” Here is how to sort it.
Generally appropriate to share
Your own thinkingPlans, drafts, frameworks, agendas, facilitation ideas. It is your work and contains nothing confidential.
Public informationPublished research, state policy, public report data. It is already in the world.
Anonymized or fictional examplesReal situations with names, IDs, and identifying details fully stripped. Remove the identity and you remove most of the risk.
General professional questions“How should I structure a coaching conversation about pacing?” No client required.
Risky or off-limits
Student names, IDs, or recordsGrades, IEP details, behavior data. Legally protected under FERPA. This is the brightest line there is. Never.
Confidential client documentsDraft strategic plans, internal data, anything shared with us in trust. It is not ours to hand to a third party without permission.
Personnel matters with namesA struggling teacher, a leadership conflict. Describe the situation generically instead; the advice will be just as good.
Unverified AI claims in deliverablesCitations, statistics, and quotes that you have not independently confirmed. AI fabricates convincingly, and the output ships under your name.

Now check your instincts

Four scenarios pulled from real consulting life. For each one, decide: safe to share with an AI tool, or hold off?

Scenario 1

You have a spreadsheet of student reading scores, with names, from a partner district. You want AI to spot trends.

Hold off. Real student names plus performance data is exactly the category that must never enter an external AI tool. The workaround is easy: strip names and IDs first, or describe the pattern in general terms and ask AI to help you build the analysis approach.
Scenario 2

You are designing a PD session on text complexity and want AI to pressure-test your draft agenda. No client names, no data, just your plan.

Safe to share. This is your own thinking with no confidential content, the top-left of the chart above. Pressure-testing your plans is one of the highest-value, lowest-risk uses of AI. Pro move: ask it what assumptions you are making and where you might lose the room.
Scenario 3

A district sent you their confidential draft strategic plan. You want AI to summarize it before tomorrow’s meeting.

Hold off. A client’s confidential document is theirs, not yours, and sharing it with a third-party tool without their knowledge breaches trust even when the tool is secure. Check your org’s current AI policy and the client agreement first. When approved enterprise tools with data protections are available, this becomes a different conversation.
Scenario 4

AI wrote you a beautiful literature review with six citations supporting your recommendation. Deadline is in an hour. Ship it to the client?

Verify first, always. AI confidently fabricates citations that look completely real: plausible authors, plausible journals, plausible years. Every source goes out under your name and TNTP’s. Check that each one exists and says what the AI claims it says.

The three-second check, before you share

  1. Does this contain real names, student data, or confidential client information?
  2. Would I be comfortable if this text appeared on a server I don’t control?
  3. Am I using AI to sharpen my expertise, or to substitute for it?

Part Five

Four Ways to Use AI as a Consultant

A practical frame for everything that follows. Every productive use of AI in our work fits one of these four modes.

AI as Search Engine

Synthesized answers, not a list of links

Orient yourself fast on unfamiliar topics. Get plain-language summaries of research, policy context, and complex reports.

For ed consultantsHeading into an MTSS meeting, ask: “Give me a plain-language summary of what research says about effective Tier 2 interventions in middle school.” Two minutes replaces forty-five minutes of skimming.
Watch out forFabricated citations. AI invents sources that look real. Use it for orientation; independently verify anything that goes in a deliverable.

AI as Thought Partner

Pushback on demand

Pressure-test ideas, surface blind spots, generate alternatives, and rehearse difficult conversations before you have them.

For ed consultantsShare a draft agenda for a PD session with a resistant staff and ask: “What assumptions am I making about this audience? Where will I lose them? What would you change and why?”
Watch out forFlattery. AI agrees by default. If you lead with “my plan is good, right?” you will get a yes. Ask it to steelman the opposing view instead.

AI as Chief of Staff

Delegate the mechanical, keep the judgment

With Claude Desktop, skills, and automations, hand off logistics: meeting prep, email drafts, calendar work, document organization, daily briefings.

For ed consultantsA morning briefing, every day, on one command: Claude checks your email and flags what needs a response, reviews today’s calendar and pulls relevant notes from previous meetings with each group, then hands you a prioritized to-do list for the day. Set it up once as a skill, and your first fifteen minutes of scattered tab-checking becomes a single two-minute read.
Watch out forAutomation without oversight, which creates errors at scale. Keep a review step before anything goes out under your name. The model works when you stay in the approval seat.

AI as Creator

Build what used to require specialists

Generate polished documents, interactive tools, dashboards, training sites, and visualizations at a quality floor most of us could not reach alone.

For ed consultantsAfter school walkthroughs, feed your notes to Claude and ask for an interactive dashboard: data by classroom, a pattern summary, a recommendations tab. A three-hour PowerPoint becomes a shareable site in thirty minutes.
Watch out forPolished-looking weak thinking. Output quality scales with input quality. AI builds the vessel; your expertise still has to fill it.

Part Six · The application block

Which one do you want to try first?

Open a tile, copy a starter prompt, and take it into Claude. Make it yours: swap in your real project, your real stuck point, your real tedious task.

Give me a plain-language summary of what the research says about [topic in your current engagement]. Include where researchers disagree.

I’m meeting with a district about [initiative]. Brief me like a smart colleague would: key concepts, common pitfalls, and three questions I should ask them.

Explain [education policy or framework] to me at three levels: one sentence, one paragraph, and one page.

Here’s my plan: [describe it]. Don’t tell me it’s good. Tell me the three biggest assumptions I’m making and what happens if each one is wrong.

I need to have a hard conversation with [role, no names] about [issue]. Role-play it with me. Play them as skeptical but reasonable.

Steelman the opposite of my position: [your position]. Make the strongest honest case against it, then tell me which counterarguments deserve the most weight.

Here are my rough notes from today’s meeting: [paste, no confidential details]. In one pass, produce all of it: a clean summary, a client-ready follow-up email, an action item list with owners and deadlines, and a draft agenda for the next meeting. I’ll review before anything goes out.

Act as my Monday morning chief of staff. Here’s everything on my plate this week: [list]. Brief me: top three priorities, what each meeting needs from me, what to batch, and what to decline. Then turn this into a reusable prompt I can paste every Monday.

Build me a coffee shop themed interactive to-do list as a single HTML file. My tasks are orders on the menu: adding a task places an order, finishing one stamps the cup. Warm cafe colors, a little personality, and it should work in any browser.

These all work in the regular Claude web app. The full version, where Claude actually reads your calendar and drafts in your inbox, is what Claude Desktop unlocks.

Build me a one-page interactive HTML resource that explains [concept] to [audience]. Clean, professional, works as a single file I can share as a link.

Turn this outline into a polished facilitation guide: [paste outline]. Include timing, talking points, and discussion questions for each segment.

Here’s anonymized data from a project: [paste]. Build an interactive dashboard with a summary view, a detail view, and a short narrative of the three biggest patterns.
